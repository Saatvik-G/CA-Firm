## Executive Overview

The Finance Act 2023 established the New Tax Regime under Section 115BAC of the Income Tax Act, 1961 as the **default tax regime** for individuals, Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs), Association of Persons (AOPs), and Body of Individuals (BOIs). For Financial Year 2024-25 (Assessment Year 2025-26), taxpayers must evaluate their income composition, permissible deductions, and long-term financial commitments before deciding whether to remain in the default regime or opt out to the Old Tax Regime.

This article provides a rigorous, factual breakdown of slab rates, allowable deductions, opt-out procedural requirements, and strategic evaluation frameworks.

---

## 1. Statutory Slab Structure under Section 115BAC

For FY 2024-25 (AY 2025-26), the revised slab structure under the default regime is as follows:

| Taxable Income Slab (₹) | Rate of Tax (%) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Up to ₹3,00,000 | Nil |
| ₹3,00,001 to ₹6,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹6,00,001 to ₹9,00,000 | 10% |
| ₹9,00,001 to ₹12,00,000 | 15% |
| ₹12,00,001 to ₹15,00,000 | 20% |
| Above ₹15,00,000 | 30% |

> [!NOTE]
> Under Section 87A, resident individuals with total income up to **₹7,00,000** are eligible for a tax rebate of up to ₹25,000 under the New Tax Regime, resulting in **zero tax liability** for income up to ₹7,00,000 (excluding marginal relief adjustments).

## 2. Permissible vs. Disallowed Deductions

### A. Permissible Deductions in New Tax Regime
Unlike its initial introduction, the revised Section 115BAC permits specified deductions:

1. **Standard Deduction on Salary**: ₹50,000 for salaried employees (increased to ₹75,000 under Finance Act 2024 proposals for FY 24-25 onwards).
2. **Employer's NPS Contribution**: Deduction under Section 80CCD(2) up to 14% of salary for Central/State Government employees and 10% (proposed 14%) for private sector employees.
3. **Transport Allowance for PwD**: Special transport allowance for specially-abled employees.
4. **Agni Veer Corpus Scheme**: Deduction under Section 80CCH(2).

### B. Major Disallowed Deductions
Taxpayers choosing the default New Tax Regime forego:

- Chapter VI-A deductions: Section 80C (PPF, ELSS, EPF, LIC), Section 80D (Health Insurance Premium), Section 80E (Education Loan Interest), Section 80G (Donations).
- House Rent Allowance (HRA) exemption under Section 10(13A).
- Leave Travel Concession (LTC) under Section 10(5).
- Interest on Self-Occupied Housing Loan under Section 24(b) up to ₹2,00,000.

## 3. Comparative Evaluation Framework

To determine the break-even threshold where the Old Tax Regime becomes more beneficial than the New Tax Regime:

$$\text{Break-Even Deductions} = \text{Total Chapter VI-A Deductions} + \text{HRA/LTC Exemptions} + \text{Housing Loan Interest}$$

For a salaried taxpayer earning **₹15,00,000**, the total tax liability under the New Tax Regime (with ₹75,000 standard deduction) is approximately **₹1,40,400** (inclusive of 4% Cess). For the Old Tax Regime to yield a lower tax liability, the taxpayer would require total eligible deductions exceeding **₹3,75,000** to **₹4,00,000**.

---

## 4. Procedural Requirements for Opting Out (Form 10-IEA)

Taxpayers deriving income from **Business or Profession** are subject to strict opt-out rules:

- **Non-Business Taxpayers (Salaried/Pensioners)**: Can choose between the Old and New regime annually at the time of filing ITR under Section 139(1).
- **Business/Professional Income Taxpayers**: Must file **Form 10-IEA** prior to the due date specified under Section 139(1). Once an opt-out choice is exercised by a business entity, they are allowed to opt back into the New Tax Regime only **once** in their lifetime.

> [!WARNING]
> Failure to submit Form 10-IEA before the due date under Section 139(1) invalidates the election of the Old Tax Regime, forcing processing under the default New Tax Regime.

The selection between tax regimes must not be based on general assumptions. A meticulous quantitative calculation comparing actual deductions against concessional slab benefits is recommended for every assessee.
